Лондон: 10 must-see достопримечательностей и секреты, которые вы не найдёте в путеводителях

Do you know these sights? Of course you do! They are known to everyone. But what about the little-known places you should visit in London? This is our article.

Gloomy slot machines

Near Lincoln's Inn Fields, there is a shop Novelty Automation, which displays the inventions of Tim Hunkin - an inventor known for his eccentric games. Hunkin is the author of a slot machine in which you need to hit the heads of bankers coming from everywhere, a divorce simulator, as well as a desktop version of Pong - one of the first computer games. Exchange money for tokens and get the opportunity to play any of these games.Visitors to Novelty Automation can exchange money for tokens, granting them access to a variety of these unique and playful inventions. Beyond Lincoln's Inn Fields, some of Hunkin's creations are also displayed in the picturesque port town of Southwold in Suffolk, further enriching the area's artistic and inventive landscape. Hunkin's games are also on display in the port town of Southwold in Suffolk.

Crossness Pumping Station

One of the two "sewage temples" through which sewage was pumped into the river during the time of Queen Victoria. The unremarkable facade of the station conceals a riot of colours inside, fig trees on the walls, and four pumps with 52-ton flywheels. The station is open several times a month, and the schedule and ticket price can be found on the website. Inside, the station houses four impressive pumps, each driven by massive 52-ton flywheels, which were vital for managing the capital's waste management before modern methods were established. These powerful machines not only represent a significant technological achievement of their time but also showcase the sheer scale and ambition of Victorian infrastructure projects. The station is open to the public several times a month, offering guided tours that delve into its history, engineering significance, and the role it played in improving public health in London.

The first coffee shop in the city

The first London coffee shop (or, more precisely, a coffee kiosk) was founded by the Greek Pasca Rose in 1652. Rose discovered the taste of coffee while serving a British merchant in Turkey. The taste of the exotic drink so impressed the Greek that he decided to bring it to London. His kiosk attracted people from all walks of life. The owners of Cornhill's pubs and taverns could only watch in envy as Pasca sold 600 cups of coffee per day. Worse still, coffee began to be portrayed as an antidote to drunkenness, violence, and lust, providing a clean mind, sophistication, and wit. Rose's coffee boom forever changed London. Rose's coffee boom forever changed London, paving the way for a vibrant café culture that would flourish in the centuries to come.
